Transmission Electron Microscope (TEM)

Transmision Electron Microscopes (TEMs) use electrons as a beam source to study small details in cells or other materials in almost atomic depth. It uses electromagnetic lenses to focus a thin beam of electrons instead of glass lenses focusing light in the light microscope. The electron beam travels through the sample. Based on the density of the material of the sample, some of the electrons get scattered. At the bottom of the microscope the unscattered electrons hit a fluorescent screen. A black and white image of the specimen is created, with its different parts displayed according to their density. The darker areas have the greatest density. The TEM offers the most powerful, a million times or more.

Samples List (in order): 1. Zinc Oxide nanoparticles 2. Chloroplast of Arabidopsis thaliana 3. Bacteriophage 4. Cultured Hippocompal Neural Axons 5. Human leukocyte (White Blood Cell)
